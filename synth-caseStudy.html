Synth: a social music discovery app

Background & Project Overview

Synth was a 10-week-long project for my course CS 377U: Understanding Users with Frank Bentley. It required us to work on a team and apply our course learnings on industry user research and design methods to the execution of a project.

In my role, I oversaw and contributed to every stage of the process, from a literature review and generative study to usability testing with an MVP and a field study with a fully functional demo app.

Problem Statement

We set out to design an application that addressed key themes and pain points uncovered in our preliminary research: the value of music as a social connector, the importance of in-person sharing, and the mixed effectiveness of traditional gamification methods. Specifically, we aimed to explore how a game-based experience could foster deeper group identity, increase music discovery diversity, and create more meaningful, socially motivated interactions.

Our goal was to build an engaging, multiplayer application that leveraged these insights to enhance the way people share and discover music together.

Process

Our process can be seperated into three phases:
1. An initial literature review, market analysis, and generative research study
2. An iterative design and development process with usability testing guiding our desgin decisions
3. The field study with our fully functional demo app

Our literature review & competitive review allowed us to first understand what others have done in the domain of music sharing and discovery, while also identifying gaps in the literature and app market. With this knowledge, we defined open research questions that we focused on in our generative research study:

  • Can gamification be leveraged to improve music discovery diversity and encourage exploration?
  • What are meaningful and motivational incentives to use when gamifying the exploration of music?
  • What specific combination of these factors can maximize user engagement & satisfaction?

Our generative study consisted of three one-on-one in-depth interviews and a diary study where three participants used the music-sharing platform Airbuds and shared their experience. After aggregating the feedback we received in our generative study, we established the following key themes:

  • People have meaningful reactions to the music that people listen to.
  • Sharing music is an important feature in music discovery.
  • In-person music sharing adds value and depth to the kinds of interactions that can be had.
  • Participants like social games but there's mixed sentiment around other forms of gamification such as badges, streaks, etc.

The above feedback guided our decision to design our app to be a social, music-sharing game that people would play in person to facilitate meaningful interactions. Enter Synth, a social music-based game that combines Spotify integration with interactive, multiplayer gameplay to facilitate music discovery and competition with your friends.

Players each contribute five recently played Spotify tracks, which are pooled and randomly selected to create a unique song for each round. In every round, a 30-second clip is played, and players must guess who listened to the song, earning points for correct guesses and optionally opening the track to save on Spotify.

As we designed and implemented our product, we performed usability tests at each phase of development (sketches, Figma, and MVP) using the feedback to address key problems on a feature-level, design/prompt-level, or overall product strategy decisions.

Our final app in its entirety is hosted on Vercel as a web application, and we were able to start our field study. We utilized multiple methods again, informational interviews, playtests, and diary studies. We tested with our target audience, college freshmen, who are often in a phase of expressed desire to meet new people and deepen their social connections.

In the front half of the field study, we ran playtests where we would observe gameplay amongst a group of 3-8 players, taking notes and doing informational interviews after. Then we chose a group of 4 friends to do a diary study with the app over a week to understand how our application is used in people's daily lives. Collecting quantitative data with Firebase Analytics and qualitative data across all of our methods, we were able to explore the larger story of synth and formulate design implications that should be addressed in the future.

Outcome

All this work culminated in our final presentation on our full product journey in front of highly regarded industry experts and a final CHI style research paper. In our presentation we got the opportunity to practice speaking about our product and process similar to how a product team might present their work. Our judges asked us questions about our process and our product.
