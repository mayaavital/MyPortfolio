Vouch: Date with a little help from your friends

Background & Project Overview

Vouch was a 6-week project for my course, CS 278: Social Computing. We worked in teams to design, launch, and manage a novel social computing system. We then analyzed the behavior we saw on our system through the lens of social computing system theories and ideas we learned throughout the course.

Problem Statement

We set out to solve the problem of how to make online dating less stressful, more authentic, and socially engaging by shifting the responsibility of matchmaking from individuals to their trusted friend groups.

Traditional dating apps often place pressure on users to make the first move and present themselves perfectly, which can be intimidating, especially in close-knit communities like Stanford. By leveraging strong social ties and group participation, our goal was to create a dating experience that felt more collaborative, inclusive, and fun, while addressing key pain points around self-presentation, engagement, and user retention.

Process

First, we drew on course concepts to choose a project topic to explore with our new sociotechnical system, we chose the frictions and social dynamics that exist among users on app-based dating sites. The main sticking points we are referring to are:

  • That having so many options at your fingertips can make dating apps feel more like an extrinsic game rather than a pursuit of an intrinsic human connection.
  • As a result of this mechanism, matches begin to pile up without either user feeling inclined to escalate this match into a real date.

To mitigate this dead end in the dating app paradigm, we wanted to create a sociotechnical system that would prioritize in-person connections and getting users on first dates. In order to test our envisioned core social dynamics without having to build any complex code, we performed a piggyback experience prototype. When building a sociotechnical system, the most important question is less about whether the interface is usable and more about nailing how the social dynamics will play out.

Piggyback Prototype

For our prototype, we acted as the matching algorithm, finding people in our community who we knew to be single, and leveraged iMessage to gauge each pair's availability, activity level, and price range for the activity that we would then choose for them. After the date ended, we interviewed each person to get their thoughts and feelings on the whole process.

In order to explore some potential different feature combinations and their effects on the social dynamics of the experience, we had a few variations in logistics across each date: single pair blind date, group blind date, and friend blind date.

From these three trials, we found that having dates planned for users generally enhanced their overall experience as it reduced decision fatigue and piqued excitement due to the blind nature. When moving to translate these insights into our final sociotechnical system, we felt we would run into significant problems trying to establish trust between online significant problems trying to establish trust between online daters choosing blind dates in a traditional dating app architecture.

In thinking more about what made our friends trust the dates we were setting them up on in the piggyback prototype, we realized that it was the fact that we, as friends, had set them up and "vouched" for the person they were meeting. This is how Vouch was born. Rather than relying solely on individual swiping, Vouch places the power of matchmaking in the hands of your social circle. Once enough of your friends and your matches' friends have approved the match, you will be prompted with a Google form to input your availability, and a blind date will be set up for you.

Design and Implementation:

Without the time to create the perfect interface design, we focused on creating a simple and intuitive experience that emphasized social matchmaking. We streamlined onboarding to avoid overwhelming users, allowing them to quickly join and explore before building out their profiles in more detail. Users could sign up as daters, matchmakers, or both, and were guided through selecting preferences such as gender and sexuality.

Once onboarded, matchmakers could swipe through candidates for their friends, locking in a friend to swipe for and voting on potential matches. We built in customization features for daters, like choosing preferred date activities and availability, which helped users coordinate matches and suggest compatible plans.

We developed the app using React Native with a backend powered by Google Firebase. This allowed us to authenticate users, store profiles, and calculate matches efficiently. Initially, we aimed to launch via TestFlight, but due to technical limitations, we pivoted to deploying a web-based version on Vercel. Despite this constraint, we successfully launched the app within the Stanford community, targeting friend groups as atomic networks to drive engagement and social accountability. While the web format may have impacted long-term retention, the technical foundation supported core functionality and enabled us to observe authentic user behavior. Features like the liaison role for coordinating dates and Google Form integration for availability were simple yet effective ways to facilitate real-life meetups through the app.

Outcome

Our launch successfully validated many of our core hypotheses about social matchmaking. After a week of use, we interviewed participants of different user types in order to gather their experiences into reflections on the design, user behavior, and social computing theory. We found that friend groups drove the most engagement and accountability on the platform. Users who joined with friends were significantly more active, regularly swiping and coordinating dates, and over only one week of use, we even observed a successful match that led to an in-person date. Our users found that the vouch process reduced the stress of self-presentation when meeting people; essentially, the difficulty of "making the first move".

However, the launch also revealed important challenges. Solo users struggled to stay engaged without a built-in network, and matchmakers expressed hesitancy when swiping for friends due to a fear of disappointment from the dater. Additionally, users were reluctant to add acquaintances as friends without clear norms on who you should be able to swipe for, and wanted more in-app communication features to collaborate during the vouching process. These insights highlighted the need for stronger onboarding, clearer expectations/norms, and more robust tools for social coordination. Moving forward, future iterations should address the introduced complexities around pressure, accountability, and trust.
